# Weather's Impact on Racing

Nothing shakes up a Formula 1 race quite like a change in weather. Rain can turn a predictable procession into pure chaos, erasing car performance advantages and putting driver skill at the forefront. Some of the most memorable races in F1 history have been wet-weather classics, and understanding how weather affects the sport helps explain why a rain cloud on the horizon sends pit walls into overdrive.

## Tire Selection: Dry, Intermediate, and Wet

F1 has three categories of tires for different conditions:

- **Dry compounds (soft, medium, hard):** Used on a completely dry track. These slick tires have no tread pattern, maximizing the rubber in contact with the surface.
- **Intermediates (green markings):** Designed for a damp track or light rain. They have a shallow tread pattern that clears small amounts of standing water while still maintaining decent grip. They are the most versatile wet-weather tire.
- **Full wet tires (blue markings):** Built for heavy rain and standing water. Their deep tread grooves can disperse approximately 85 liters of water per second at top speed.

Choosing the right tire at the right moment is one of the most impactful strategic decisions a team can make. Switch to intermediates too early on a drying track and you gain a huge advantage. Wait too long and you lose seconds per lap to rivals who made the call first.

## When Races Go Wet

Rain can arrive before the race starts or develop mid-race. Each scenario plays out differently.

If heavy rain is present before the start, race control may declare a **wet start**. In extreme cases, the start is delayed entirely while conditions are assessed. The 2021 Belgian Grand Prix at Spa was the most infamous example, where persistent heavy rain meant only a handful of laps were completed behind the Safety Car before the race was stopped.

When rain arrives during a dry race, the opening laps on the wrong tires are intense. Dry slick tires have zero water-clearing ability, so cars on slicks in the rain have massively reduced grip. The pit lane becomes frantic as teams scramble to bring drivers in for the right rubber. Timing this switch is critical. Pitting one lap too late can mean a spin or a crash.

## Red Flags and Suspended Races

If conditions become too dangerous to continue, race control will show the **red flag**, stopping the race entirely. Cars return to the pit lane, and the session is suspended until conditions improve. Red flags are relatively rare but tend to happen when visibility drops so low that drivers cannot see the car directly ahead, or when standing water creates an unacceptable aquaplaning risk.

When a red flag is lifted, the race restarts either from a standing start or a rolling start behind the Safety Car, depending on conditions. Recent regulation changes have favored standing restarts after red flags, adding another layer of unpredictability.

## Formation Laps Behind the Safety Car

In wet conditions, the race director may choose to start the race behind the **Safety Car** rather than with a normal standing start. The field circulates at reduced speed while conditions are evaluated. This prevents the dangerous spray and limited visibility of a standing start in heavy rain.

During Safety Car laps, teams gather information about track conditions from their drivers. "How much standing water is there?" and "Can you see the car ahead?" are common radio questions. Once the race director is satisfied, the Safety Car peels into the pit lane and racing begins.

These laps do count toward the total race distance, so a prolonged Safety Car start reduces the number of racing laps.

## Track Evolution and the Drying Line

After rain stops, the track doesn't dry evenly. A **drying line** forms first on the racing line itself, where cars' tires push water aside and the heat from the rubber and brakes helps evaporate moisture. This narrow strip of drier tarmac gradually widens over successive laps.

Drivers must decide whether to stay on the drying line (which is slower in corners because it follows the standard racing line, not the optimal wet line) or take a different, wetter path that might offer better geometry through a corner.

As the drying line expands, teams face the decision of when to switch from wet or intermediate tires to dry slicks. Go too early and the slick tires won't generate enough heat on the remaining damp patches, causing slides and potential crashes. Get the timing right, and you can gain enormous time over rivals still on grooved rubber.

Track temperature, wind speed, and cloud cover all affect drying speed. A windy, warm day after a rain shower can dry a track in 10 to 15 laps. An overcast, cool day can keep the surface damp for much longer.

## Rain Specialists

Some drivers have built legendary reputations for their wet-weather ability. **Ayrton Senna** is often considered the greatest rain driver in history, with his 1984 Monaco Grand Prix drive in the Toleman and his 1993 Donington Park masterclass standing as defining performances.

**Lewis Hamilton** has delivered numerous wet-weather victories, including his breakthrough win at the 2008 British Grand Prix at Silverstone. **Max Verstappen** announced himself to the world with a stunning wet drive at the 2016 Brazilian Grand Prix, dancing through the spray in a way that drew direct comparisons to Senna.

What makes a great rain driver? Superior car feel, the ability to sense the grip limit on a constantly changing surface, and the confidence to push when others back off. Wet conditions narrow the performance gap between cars and widen the gap between drivers. When it rains, talent rises to the surface.
