# The Business of F1

Formula 1 is one of the most commercially valuable sports properties on the planet. The series generates over $3 billion in annual revenue, attracts global broadcast audiences of around 1.5 billion cumulative viewers per season, and involves teams that operate on budgets exceeding $100 million each. Behind the on-track competition is a complex business structure that determines who gets paid, how much teams can spend, and what it takes to join the club.

## FOM and the Revenue Split

The commercial rights to Formula 1 are held by the **Formula One Group (FOM)**, which manages race hosting deals, broadcast rights, sponsorship, and digital products. FOM's revenue comes from three primary sources:

- **Race hosting fees:** Each circuit pays FOM a fee to host a Grand Prix, typically between $25 million and $65 million per year. Street races and races in the Middle East tend to pay the highest fees. Traditional European circuits like Monza and Spa often negotiate lower rates due to their historical significance.
- **Broadcast rights:** TV and streaming deals with global broadcasters generate hundreds of millions annually. F1 TV, the sport's own streaming platform, adds a growing direct-to-consumer revenue stream.
- **Sponsorship and advertising:** Title sponsors, trackside advertising, and paddock club hospitality contribute significant income.

FOM distributes a portion of its revenue to the teams through a tiered system. Teams receive payments based on their constructors' championship finishing position, with historical bonuses for certain teams (Ferrari famously receives a "Long Standing Team" bonus worth tens of millions). This revenue sharing is governed by the Concorde Agreement.

## Team Budgets and the Budget Cap

Before 2021, F1 had no spending limits. Top teams like Mercedes, Ferrari, and Red Bull spent upward of $400 million per season on car development, while smaller teams operated on less than a quarter of that. The performance gap was often a direct reflection of spending power.

The **budget cap**, introduced in 2021, limits each team's spending to $135 million per season (as of the most recent regulations, with adjustments for inflation). This cap covers car development, manufacturing, and race operations. It does not include driver salaries, the top three highest-paid personnel, marketing, or power unit costs.

The cap has narrowed the competitive field. Midfield teams can now compete more effectively knowing that the wealthiest teams cannot simply outspend them without limit. However, enforcement has been contentious. Red Bull exceeded the 2021 cap by approximately $1.8 million, resulting in a $7 million fine and a 10% reduction in their wind tunnel and CFD allowances.

## Sponsorship Tiers

F1 team sponsorship operates across several tiers:

- **Title sponsors** pay the most and have their name attached to the team (Oracle Red Bull Racing, Scuderia Ferrari HP). These deals can be worth $50 million to over $100 million per year.
- **Major partners** appear on prominent areas of the car (sidepods, rear wing) and typically pay $10 million to $30 million annually.
- **Official partners and suppliers** contribute smaller amounts or provide products and services in kind (fuel, lubricants, software, logistics).

Teams also negotiate individual driver sponsorship deals, helmet branding, and social media campaigns. The total sponsorship income for a top team can exceed $200 million per season.

F1's growth in the United States, driven partly by the Netflix series "Drive to Survive," has attracted major American sponsors including Salesforce, Google, and various crypto and tech firms.

## Hosting Fees and the Race Calendar

Countries and cities pay substantial fees to host a Grand Prix. These fees fund FOM's operations and contribute to team prize money. The calendar has expanded to 24 races per season, with hosting fees generating over $700 million annually for FOM.

New races in Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Las Vegas, and Miami command premium hosting fees, often backed by government investment aimed at tourism and global visibility. Meanwhile, historic races at tracks like Spa-Francorchamps and Monza face regular uncertainty over contract renewals because their governments are less willing to subsidize the fees.

The hosting fee model is a source of ongoing debate. Critics argue it prices out iconic venues in favor of wealthy nations willing to pay for prestige.

## Driver Salaries

F1 driver salaries are not covered by the budget cap and vary enormously. Top drivers earn between $40 million and $55 million annually (Max Verstappen's contract is widely reported as the highest at approximately $55 million per year). Midfield and rookie drivers earn between $1 million and $10 million, with some rookies earning as little as $500,000 in their debut seasons.

Beyond base salary, drivers earn performance bonuses for wins, podiums, and championship positions. They also have personal sponsorship deals, appearance fees, and licensing income.

## Liberty Media

**Liberty Media Corporation** acquired Formula 1 in 2017 for approximately $4.4 billion. Under Liberty's ownership, the sport has expanded aggressively into new markets, particularly the United States. Liberty introduced the Las Vegas and Miami Grands Prix, invested heavily in digital and social media (F1's social following has grown to over 90 million across platforms), and supported the "Drive to Survive" documentary series that brought millions of new fans to the sport.

Liberty operates F1 through the Formula One Group, publicly traded on the NASDAQ under the ticker FWONA/FWONK. The company's strategy focuses on growing the calendar, increasing broadcast revenue, and building F1's brand beyond traditional motorsport audiences.

## The Concorde Agreement

The **Concorde Agreement** is the contract that binds the FIA, FOM, and all F1 teams together. It governs prize money distribution, commercial rights, governance structures, and the teams' commitment to compete in the championship.

The current agreement runs through 2025, with negotiations for the next cycle already underway. Key points of contention typically include the revenue split between FOM and teams, the structure of bonus payments, and governance (how much say teams have in regulation changes).

Historically, Ferrari has held a unique position in Concorde negotiations, including a veto power over certain regulation changes, a reflection of their status as the only team to have competed in every F1 World Championship season since 1950.

## New Team Entry Process

Joining the F1 grid is extraordinarily difficult and expensive. Prospective new entrants must apply to the FIA, demonstrate financial stability, present a credible technical plan, and pay an entry fee of $200 million (a "dilution fund" distributed to existing teams to compensate for their reduced share of prize money).

The most recent successful new entry was approved in 2023 when the FIA accepted a bid backed by General Motors/Cadillac, though the team's actual grid debut is planned for 2026. Previous attempts by Andretti Autosport faced resistance from existing teams who were reluctant to share revenue with an 11th team.

The high barriers to entry protect existing teams' commercial interests but also limit growth. The grid has been fixed at 10 teams (20 cars) for over a decade, and expanding it remains a contentious topic in the paddock.
