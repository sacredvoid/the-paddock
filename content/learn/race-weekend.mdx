# How a Race Weekend Works

A Formula 1 race weekend is a carefully structured three-day event that builds from practice sessions through qualifying and into the main race. Understanding the weekend format helps you follow the action from the very first session.

## Friday: Practice Sessions

The weekend begins with two free practice sessions (FP1 and FP2), each lasting 60 minutes. Teams use these sessions to gather data, test setups, and let drivers learn the track. FP1 is often used to give rookie or reserve drivers seat time, as regulations require teams to run a non-regular driver in at least two FP1 sessions per season.

During practice, engineers collect tyre data, fuel load readings, and aero performance numbers. Drivers test different car setups, comparing mechanical grip versus downforce configurations. The lap times in practice rarely reflect true pace since teams run different fuel loads and tyre compounds.

## Saturday: Final Practice and Qualifying

Saturday morning brings FP3, the final 60-minute practice session. Teams fine-tune their setups ahead of qualifying and run through their planned qualifying simulations.

Qualifying takes place on Saturday afternoon and determines the starting grid for the race. It uses a knockout format split into three segments:

- **Q1 (18 minutes):** All 20 drivers compete. The five slowest are eliminated and fill grid positions 16 through 20.
- **Q2 (15 minutes):** The remaining 15 drivers compete. Five more are eliminated, filling positions 11 through 15.
- **Q3 (12 minutes):** The top 10 drivers fight for pole position. The fastest driver starts the race from P1.

Each segment resets, so drivers must set competitive lap times in every phase to advance.

## Sprint Weekends

Six races per season follow a modified Sprint format. On these weekends, qualifying moves to Friday afternoon, and a shorter Sprint Qualifying (SQ) session takes place on Saturday morning to set the grid for the Sprint race. The Sprint itself is a roughly 100 km race held on Saturday afternoon, awarding points to the top eight finishers (8-7-6-5-4-3-2-1). The original qualifying result still determines the grid for Sunday's Grand Prix.

Sprint weekends condense the schedule and remove one practice session, so teams arrive with less time to experiment with setups.

## Sunday: Race Day

Race day is the main event. The typical schedule includes a drivers' parade, a formation lap, and then the race itself, which covers a set number of laps designed to total roughly 305 km (with Monaco being the exception at around 260 km).

### Formation Lap

Before the race starts, all cars complete a formation lap behind the pole-sitter. During this lap, drivers weave to warm their tyres and brakes. They then line up on the grid in their qualifying positions.

### The Start

Five red lights appear one by one on the gantry above the start/finish straight. When all five are lit, they extinguish simultaneously, and the race begins. The opening lap is usually the most dramatic, with cars jostling for position into the first corners.

### During the Race

Teams must make at least one pit stop, since regulations require the use of at least two different dry tyre compounds. Strategy plays a massive role: when to pit, which tyres to choose, and how to respond to safety cars can make or break a race result.

### Chequered Flag

The race ends when the leader crosses the finish line after completing the full race distance. The chequered flag is shown, and all other cars complete their current lap before the session ends. Points are awarded from first through tenth place, and the podium ceremony follows with the top three drivers and the winning constructor celebrated.

## Why the Format Matters

Every session feeds into the next. Practice informs qualifying strategy. Qualifying position shapes race strategy. Understanding this progression turns a race weekend from a single Sunday event into three days of unfolding competition.
