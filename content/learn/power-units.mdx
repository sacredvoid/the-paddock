# Engine & Power Unit Basics

Formula 1 hasn't used a simple engine since 2013. Since 2014, every car on the grid runs a **power unit (PU)**, a combination of a turbocharged internal combustion engine and two electric motor-generators. The total system produces around 1,000 horsepower from just 1.6 liters of displacement, making F1 power units the most thermally efficient internal combustion engines ever built.

## The V6 Turbo Hybrid

The core of the power unit is a 1.6-liter **V6 turbocharged engine**, with the cylinders arranged at a fixed 90-degree angle. It revs to a maximum of 15,000 RPM (though in practice, most teams run closer to 12,500 RPM for reliability and fuel efficiency). The turbocharger compresses incoming air to increase power output, recovering energy that would otherwise be lost as exhaust heat.

These engines achieve thermal efficiency above 50%, meaning more than half the energy in the fuel is converted to useful work. For comparison, a typical road car engine manages around 30-35%. This efficiency is one of the defining technical achievements of the current F1 era.

## MGU-K: Kinetic Energy Recovery

The **MGU-K (Motor Generator Unit - Kinetic)** is an electric motor connected to the crankshaft. Under braking, it acts as a generator, converting the car's kinetic energy into electrical energy and storing it in the battery. Under acceleration, it reverses and deploys that stored energy as additional power.

The MGU-K can harvest up to 2 MJ (megajoules) of energy per lap and deploy up to 4 MJ per lap (the extra energy comes from the MGU-H). When deploying, it adds approximately 120 kW (about 161 horsepower) to the engine's output. Drivers typically feel this as a seamless boost on corner exits and down straights.

The concept is similar to regenerative braking in a road-going hybrid or electric car, but operating at far higher power levels and with much faster charge/discharge cycles.

## MGU-H: Heat Energy Recovery

The **MGU-H (Motor Generator Unit - Heat)** is the component that sets F1 power units apart from any other hybrid system in the world. It sits between the turbine and compressor of the turbocharger and performs two functions.

First, it recovers energy from exhaust gases that spin the turbine. Instead of all that energy simply spinning the compressor, the MGU-H captures a portion and sends it to the battery or directly to the MGU-K.

Second, it can act as a motor to spin the compressor independently of exhaust flow. This eliminates **turbo lag**, the delay between a driver pressing the throttle and the turbo building enough boost. When the driver gets on the power, the MGU-H instantly spools the compressor, delivering immediate response. This is why modern F1 cars feel so responsive despite being turbocharged.

The MGU-H is also the most complex and expensive component in the power unit, which is why it is being removed from the next generation of regulations in 2026.

## The Energy Store

The **Energy Store (ES)** is essentially the battery pack. It stores electrical energy harvested by the MGU-K and MGU-H and releases it when needed. The minimum weight is 20 kg, and its maximum stored energy capacity is 4 MJ.

The battery must manage enormous charge and discharge rates while staying within strict temperature limits. Overheating the battery can force teams to reduce deployment, costing several tenths per lap. Battery management is an ongoing engineering challenge, particularly at hot races like Bahrain or Qatar.

## Fuel Flow Limit

F1 doesn't just limit how much fuel a car can carry (110 kg for a race). It also limits the **fuel flow rate** to a maximum of 100 kg per hour above 10,500 RPM. This prevents teams from simply burning more fuel to make more power, forcing them to extract maximum performance from every drop.

The fuel flow limit is a key reason why thermal efficiency has been pushed so far. With a fixed fuel budget, the only way to go faster is to convert more of that fuel's energy into forward motion.

The FIA monitors fuel flow in real time using ultrasonic sensors. Any breach results in immediate investigation and potential disqualification, as McLaren learned when Daniel Ricciardo's car was excluded from the 2014 Australian Grand Prix for a fuel flow violation.

## ICE vs. Full Power Unit

It's worth understanding the distinction between the **ICE (Internal Combustion Engine)** alone and the complete power unit. The ICE refers specifically to the V6 turbo, producing roughly 850 horsepower by itself. The full power unit includes the ICE plus both MGUs, the turbocharger, the energy store, and the control electronics, bringing total output to around 1,000 horsepower.

Each driver is allocated a limited number of power unit components per season. Exceeding the allocation triggers grid penalties. For 2024, each driver gets four ICEs, four turbochargers, four MGU-Hs, four MGU-Ks, two energy stores, and two control electronics units for the full season. Strategic management of these components is a constant factor in team planning.

## Manufacturer vs. Customer Teams

Only four manufacturers currently produce F1 power units: **Mercedes**, **Ferrari**, **Renault** (Alpine), and **Red Bull Powertrains/Honda**. These manufacturers supply their own works teams and also provide units to customer teams under commercial agreements.

Customer teams receive identical power unit hardware to the manufacturer's own team. This is mandated by the regulations, so a customer like Williams receives the same Mercedes power unit that the Mercedes works team uses. However, the works team often has advantages in integration, with the car's chassis designed hand-in-hand with the power unit from the earliest concept stages.

Customer contracts typically run for multiple years and cost in the range of 15 to 20 million dollars per season. For smaller teams, this is a significant expense, but building a power unit from scratch would cost far more (estimated at 100+ million dollars annually).

From 2026, new power unit regulations will increase the electrical component's contribution, remove the MGU-H, and introduce sustainable fuels. These changes are designed to attract new manufacturers, with Audi confirmed as a new entrant for that season.
