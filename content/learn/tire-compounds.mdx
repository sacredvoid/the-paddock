# Tire Compounds Explained

Tires are one of the most important variables in Formula 1. The compound a team selects directly affects grip, speed, and how long a stint lasts before a pit stop is needed. Pirelli, F1's sole tire supplier since 2011, provides five distinct compounds for race weekends.

## The Dry Compounds

For dry conditions, Pirelli brings three compounds to each race, selected from a range of five. They are always labeled using three colors regardless of which specific compounds are chosen:

- **Soft (Red):** Maximum grip but the fastest to degrade. Used primarily in qualifying for the quickest single-lap pace, and in race stints where short bursts of speed are needed.
- **Medium (Yellow):** The balanced compound. Offers a good mix of performance and durability. Often the go-to tire for the first or final stint of a race.
- **Hard (White):** The most durable compound with the lowest grip. Slower per lap but can run significantly longer, making it valuable for one-stop strategies.

The actual rubber compounds vary from race to race. What's labeled "soft" at Monaco (a low-energy circuit) might be a completely different compound than the "soft" brought to Silverstone (a high-energy circuit). Pirelli selects the three best-suited compounds for each track based on surface roughness, temperatures, and corner speeds.

## The Wet Compounds

When rain arrives, two additional tire types come into play:

- **Intermediates (Green):** Designed for a damp track or light rain. These tires have a tread pattern that clears water while still providing reasonable grip. They can also work on a drying track as the water clears.
- **Full Wets (Blue):** Built for heavy rain. Their deep tread channels can disperse roughly 85 liters of water per second at full speed. They provide maximum water clearance but are slower on a drier surface due to their increased rolling resistance.

## The Grip vs. Degradation Tradeoff

Every tire choice involves a tradeoff. Softer compounds generate more grip because their rubber is stickier and conforms better to the track surface. But that stickiness also means the rubber wears away faster, causing lap times to increase as the tire degrades.

Harder compounds sacrifice outright grip for longevity. A driver on hard tires might be a second slower per lap, but they can run 15 to 20 laps longer before needing fresh rubber. Over the course of a full race, that time saved in the pits can outweigh the per-lap deficit.

Tire management is a crucial driver skill. Smooth inputs, careful throttle application, and avoiding unnecessary sliding can extend tire life significantly. Some drivers, like former champion Fernando Alonso, built reputations around exceptional tire management.

## Mandatory Pit Stop Rules

Regulations require every driver to use at least two different dry tire compounds during a race. This guarantees at least one pit stop and prevents teams from simply bolting on the hardest compound and running the entire distance without stopping.

If a race starts in wet conditions and stays wet throughout, this rule is suspended since intermediate and full wet tires are distinct from the dry compounds.

## Why Tires Matter So Much

Tires are the only point of contact between a 900 kg car and the track surface. The contact patch for each tire is roughly the size of a postcard. Everything the car does - accelerating, braking, cornering - flows through those four small patches of rubber. Understanding tire strategy is understanding one of the most critical layers of F1 competition.
