# Multi 21: Vettel's Betrayal

On March 24, 2013, the Malaysian Grand Prix produced one of the most uncomfortable scenes in modern Formula 1. A reigning triple world champion ignored a direct team order, stole a race victory from his teammate, and triggered a public fallout that exposed the tensions simmering inside one of the sport's most successful teams. The code was simple. The betrayal was brazen. And the phrase "Multi 21" became permanently etched into F1 lore.

## The Context

**Red Bull Racing** had dominated Formula 1 from 2010 to 2013 with **Sebastian Vettel** winning four consecutive world championships. But the team's second driver, **Mark Webber**, was no pushover. The Australian was fast, experienced, and fiercely independent. He had won multiple Grands Prix and had fought Vettel for the 2010 championship down to the final race.

The relationship between the two was tense. Webber felt the team consistently favored Vettel with upgrades and strategy. Vettel saw Webber as a teammate he had clearly outperformed and expected to beat. Red Bull team principal **Christian Horner** and chief technical officer **Adrian Newey** tried to manage the dynamic, but trust between the drivers had been eroding for years.

By 2013, Webber had announced he would leave F1 at the end of the season. It was his final year. And then came Malaysia.

## The Code

**"Multi 21"** was a Red Bull team code. The numbers referred to car numbers: car 2 (Webber) finishes ahead of car 1 (Vettel). "Multi 12" meant the reverse. When the team radioed "Multi 21" to both drivers, it was a clear instruction: hold position, Webber stays ahead, Vettel stays behind. The race was won. Stop fighting.

## The Race

The 2013 Malaysian Grand Prix was held in brutal tropical heat at the Sepang International Circuit. As the race entered its final phase, Webber led with Vettel second. Both Red Bulls were comfortably ahead of the rest of the field. The team had what they wanted: a 1-2 finish.

The order came through. "Multi 21, Seb. Multi 21." The instruction was unambiguous. Vettel was told to turn down his engine, conserve his tyres, and follow Webber home. Webber received the same message and complied, reducing his pace and switching to a fuel-saving mode.

Vettel did not comply.

Instead of backing off, Vettel pushed harder. He attacked Webber, who was now driving conservatively as instructed. The two cars fought wheel-to-wheel for several intense laps. Webber, caught off guard and running in a lower engine mode, tried to defend. But Vettel was relentless. He forced his way past and pulled away, winning the race.

## The Aftermath

The scenes after the race were extraordinary. On the podium, Webber was visibly furious. When asked about the battle, he responded with barely contained anger: **"Multi 21, Seb. Multi 21."** The phrase was delivered as an accusation, dripping with contempt. It instantly became one of the most quoted radio messages in F1 history.

In the post-race press conference, Webber made no attempt to hide his feelings. "In the end, Seb made his own decisions today and will have protection as usual," he said, a barely veiled reference to his belief that Red Bull would never seriously punish their golden boy.

Vettel, for his part, showed brief remorse. In the immediate aftermath, he appeared uncomfortable. In the team debrief, he reportedly apologized. But his public statements were more ambiguous. "I'm not here to finish second," he told media. "I'm a racing driver."

Christian Horner tried to manage the fallout. He publicly criticized Vettel, saying the team order should have been respected. Horner called it "unacceptable" and said the situation would be dealt with internally. Red Bull motorsport advisor **Helmut Marko** was less diplomatic, essentially backing Vettel and suggesting that the faster driver should always win.

## The Deeper Rift

Multi 21 did not happen in isolation. It was the culmination of years of friction. Webber pointed to the 2012 Brazilian Grand Prix, where Vettel had received strategic advantages in a championship-deciding race. He pointed to the so-called "new front wing" incident in 2010, when a prototype wing was taken from Webber's car and given to Vettel. He pointed to a pattern of treatment that he felt consistently undermined him.

For Webber, Malaysia 2013 was confirmation of everything he had suspected. The team may have publicly scolded Vettel, but there were no material consequences. No points deduction. No grid penalty. No suspension. The message, as Webber saw it, was clear: Vettel could do what he wanted.

## The Season Plays Out

Vettel went on to dominate the 2013 season in historic fashion, winning 13 races including a record nine consecutive victories to close out the year. Webber finished third in the championship and left F1 at the end of the season to race in the World Endurance Championship.

The two were professional for the remainder of the year, but the warmth was gone. Team radio messages were terse. Podium celebrations were stiff. The partnership that had delivered four constructors' titles limped to its end.

## Legacy

Multi 21 became shorthand for a specific kind of betrayal in F1: a driver putting personal ambition above team loyalty. It raised questions that the sport still grapples with. Should drivers always obey team orders? Is a racing driver's instinct to win something that can (or should) be overridden by corporate strategy? What does a team owe a departing driver in his final season?

Vettel himself seemed to evolve on the subject. In later years, particularly during his time at Ferrari and Aston Martin, he became known as one of the most team-oriented and respectful drivers on the grid. He spoke about maturity and perspective. He never fully disowned what happened in Malaysia, but he clearly grew beyond it.

Webber, in retirement, eventually made peace with the situation. The two have shared cordial moments in the paddock in the years since. But whenever a team order is given and a driver hesitates, whenever a radio message goes ignored, the paddock remembers. Multi 21, Seb. Multi 21.
