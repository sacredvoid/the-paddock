# Team Orders Gone Wrong

Formula 1 has always lived with an uncomfortable tension: it is a team sport where two drivers share a garage but only one can win. **Team orders**, instructions from the pit wall dictating which driver should finish ahead, have been part of racing since its earliest days. Sometimes they work smoothly. Sometimes they produce moments of fury, humiliation, and mutiny that the sport never forgets.

## Austria 2002: "Let Michael Pass"

The 2002 Austrian Grand Prix is the defining example of team orders gone wrong, not because the driver disobeyed, but because he obeyed too visibly.

**Rubens Barrichello** had driven a superb race at the A1-Ring, leading from the front in his **Ferrari**. With the finish line in sight, the team radio crackled with an instruction: let **Michael Schumacher** through. Barrichello slowed on the final straight and yielded the lead just meters before the line. Schumacher swept past to "win."

The reaction was immediate and hostile. The crowd booed throughout the podium ceremony. Schumacher, visibly uncomfortable, tried to push Barrichello onto the top step of the podium, but the gesture only made things worse. It highlighted the absurdity of what had just happened. Ferrari was fined $1 million for bringing the sport into disrepute.

The outrage was amplified by context. Schumacher was already dominating the 2002 season so comprehensively that the championship was barely competitive. He did not need this result. The extra points were meaningless. Ferrari's insistence on maximizing Schumacher's tally at Barrichello's expense felt needlessly cruel.

The incident prompted the **FIA to ban team orders** from 2003 onward, inserting Article 39.1 into the Sporting Regulations: "Team orders which interfere with a race result are prohibited." It was a rule born of embarrassment, and, as future events would show, nearly impossible to enforce.

## Germany 2010: "Fernando Is Faster Than You"

Eight years after Austria, Ferrari found themselves at the center of another team orders storm. At the **2010 German Grand Prix** at Hockenheim, **Felipe Massa** was leading the race with **Fernando Alonso** close behind. Ferrari's race engineer Rob Smedley radioed Massa with a message that would become legendary: **"Fernando is faster than you. Can you confirm you understood that message?"**

Massa understood. He slowed and let Alonso through. The delivery was awkward, the intent was transparent, and the timing was terrible. Team orders were still officially banned. Ferrari had just issued one on live television, broadcast to millions.

The stewards investigated and fined Ferrari $100,000, a trivial amount for a team of Ferrari's resources. The FIA acknowledged that enforcing the team orders ban was effectively impossible. How do you prove that a driver slowing down was an order rather than a personal decision? How do you distinguish between "Fernando is faster than you" and genuine race information?

The farce of trying to police team orders led the FIA to **lift the ban entirely in 2011**. Teams were once again free to issue orders, provided they were not considered dangerous. The reasoning was pragmatic: teams were doing it anyway through coded messages and strategic manipulation. Better to allow it openly than pretend it wasn't happening.

Massa, for his part, was devastated. He never challenged for a championship again, and the German Grand Prix marked a turning point in his Ferrari career. The loyal number two had been loyal one too many times.

## Malaysia 2013: "Multi 21, Seb"

Red Bull's "Multi 21" debacle at the 2013 Malaysian Grand Prix was different from the Ferrari incidents because the order was **ignored rather than obeyed**. When **Sebastian Vettel** attacked and passed teammate **Mark Webber** despite clear instructions to hold position, it was an act of open defiance.

Webber's post-race fury, the icy podium, and Vettel's half-hearted apology created one of the most uncomfortable team moments in modern F1. Red Bull publicly condemned Vettel but imposed no meaningful punishment, confirming Webber's long-held suspicion that the team's hierarchy favored the younger German.

The incident proved that team orders cut both ways. Ferrari's problem was drivers who obeyed too readily. Red Bull's problem was a driver who refused to obey at all.

## Russia 2018: "Valtteri, It's James"

**Mercedes** had generally handled their driver dynamics well during the Hamilton-Rosberg era (with notable exceptions), but the 2018 Russian Grand Prix exposed the limits of corporate team management.

**Valtteri Bottas** led the race at Sochi, with **Lewis Hamilton** second. Hamilton was locked in a championship battle with Ferrari's Sebastian Vettel. Mercedes decided that Hamilton needed the extra points more than Bottas and ordered the swap. Team strategist **James Vowles** delivered the message: **"Valtteri, it's James. Please hold position."**

Bottas complied, slowing to let Hamilton through. Unlike Ferrari's clumsy attempts to disguise their orders, Mercedes was direct. They acknowledged what they were doing and explained the championship logic behind it.

The honesty did not prevent the backlash. Fans were sympathetic to Bottas, who had driven the better race and deserved the victory. Hamilton won but seemed uneasy about how it was achieved. Mercedes promised to "repay" Bottas when the opportunity arose, but the opportunity never really came in a way that felt equivalent.

Bottas's radio silence after the order spoke volumes. He obeyed, as he always did, but each obedience cost something. His time at Mercedes was defined by moments like these: fast enough to lead races, but never allowed to win when it mattered to the team's championship arithmetic.

## Brazil 2022: "Perez Has No Right"

The 2022 season finale at **Interlagos** produced the most recent team orders controversy, and one of the most bitter. **Max Verstappen**, already crowned champion, was asked by Red Bull to let teammate **Sergio Perez** pass in the final laps. Perez was fighting for second in the championship against Ferrari's Charles Leclerc, and the extra points would help.

Verstappen refused. **"I told you already last time. Don't ask that again. Are we clear? I gave my reasons, and I stand by it,"** he radioed to his engineer. The message was blunt, almost hostile. Verstappen had his own grievances with Perez, reportedly related to an incident in qualifying at the Monaco Grand Prix earlier that year.

Red Bull was blindsided on live television. Perez finished behind Verstappen and ultimately lost second in the championship to Leclerc by two points, exactly the margin that a position swap in Brazil would have covered.

The fallout was significant. Verstappen faced intense criticism, particularly in Mexico, where Perez is a national hero. Red Bull tried to smooth things over during the off-season, but the relationship between the drivers was clearly damaged. Team principal Christian Horner admitted the situation "wasn't ideal."

## The Impossible Balance

Team orders will always be part of Formula 1 because the sport's structure demands it. Two drivers share resources, strategy, and a garage, but only one can win. When a team has a clear number one in a title fight, the math is simple: maximize the leader's points.

But simple math clashes with human emotion. Drivers are competitors first. Asking one to sacrifice for another goes against every instinct that got them to Formula 1 in the first place. The fans, too, want genuine racing, not choreographed results.

Every team order controversy reinforces the same lesson: there is no clean way to do this. Obey, and you lose your dignity. Disobey, and you lose your team's trust. The pit wall asks for cooperation. The driver's heart demands victory. Something always breaks.
