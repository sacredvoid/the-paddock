# The Points System

Formula 1 uses a points-based system to determine both the Drivers' World Championship and the Constructors' World Championship. Points are awarded based on finishing position in each Grand Prix and Sprint race throughout the season.

## Race Points

The top ten finishers in each Grand Prix receive points on the following scale:

| Position | Points |
|----------|--------|
| 1st | 25 |
| 2nd | 18 |
| 3rd | 15 |
| 4th | 12 |
| 5th | 10 |
| 6th | 8 |
| 7th | 6 |
| 8th | 4 |
| 9th | 2 |
| 10th | 1 |

Drivers finishing outside the top ten receive zero points but are still classified as finishers if they complete at least 90% of the race distance.

## Fastest Lap Bonus

One additional point is awarded to the driver who sets the fastest lap of the race, but only if that driver finishes in the top ten. This rule prevents backmarkers from pitting for fresh tires near the end of a race solely to grab the bonus point, since they wouldn't score unless they're already in a points-paying position.

The fastest lap point was reintroduced in 2019 after being absent from the regulations for decades. It adds a strategic element to the closing stages of races, as teams weigh whether to make a late pit stop for fresh tires to chase the extra point.

## Sprint Points

Six weekends per season feature a Sprint race, a shorter event held on Saturday. Points are awarded to the top eight finishers:

| Position | Points |
|----------|--------|
| 1st | 8 |
| 2nd | 7 |
| 3rd | 6 |
| 4th | 5 |
| 5th | 4 |
| 6th | 3 |
| 7th | 2 |
| 8th | 1 |

No fastest lap point is awarded in Sprint races. The Sprint results do not affect the starting grid for Sunday's Grand Prix.

## The Drivers' Championship

The Drivers' World Championship is determined by the total points accumulated by each driver across all races and sprints in the season. Every point counts, and the championship often comes down to consistency rather than outright wins. A driver who finishes second in every race will outscore a driver who wins half the races but retires from the other half.

If two drivers are tied on points at the end of the season, the tiebreaker goes to the driver with more race wins. If wins are also equal, the comparison moves to second-place finishes, then thirds, and so on down the order.

## The Constructors' Championship

The Constructors' World Championship combines the points scored by both drivers of each team. This championship determines the financial distribution from F1's prize fund, making it extremely valuable. The difference between finishing third and fourth in the Constructors' standings can be worth tens of millions of dollars.

Because both drivers contribute, teams need consistent scoring from both sides of the garage. A team with one dominant driver and one who regularly finishes outside the points will struggle against a rival with two solid points scorers.

## Why Points Matter Beyond the Title

Championship position affects more than just the trophy. It determines prize money, commercial rights income, wind tunnel and CFD development time (lower-placed teams get more testing allowance to help them catch up), and even garage placement in the pit lane. The points system is the foundation that shapes nearly every competitive and financial decision in Formula 1.
