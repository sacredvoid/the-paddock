# Pit Stop Strategy

A Formula 1 race is rarely won on raw speed alone. Strategy, specifically when and how a team uses its pit stops, often determines who stands on the top step of the podium. The best strategists can turn a car that qualifies fifth into a race winner through smart decisions in the pit lane.

## What Happens During a Pit Stop

A modern F1 pit stop is a marvel of precision. When a car enters the pit lane, it must observe the speed limit (typically 80 km/h, or 60 km/h at street circuits). The car stops on its marks in front of the garage, and a crew of roughly 20 mechanics descends on the car simultaneously:

- **Three mechanics per wheel:** One operates the wheel gun to loosen and tighten the nut, one removes the old tire, and one fits the new tire.
- **Front and rear jack operators:** Lift the car so the wheels can be changed.
- **Stabilizers:** Hold the car steady during the stop.

The entire tire change takes roughly 2 to 3 seconds for the top teams. The world record is under 2 seconds. Including the pit lane entry and exit, a pit stop costs approximately 20 to 25 seconds of total race time compared to staying on track.

## The Undercut

The undercut is one of the most common strategic moves in F1. Here's how it works:

1. Car A is running behind Car B on track.
2. Car A pits first, switching to fresh tires.
3. On fresh rubber, Car A sets significantly faster lap times than Car B, who is still running on worn tires.
4. When Car B finally pits, they emerge behind Car A, having lost the position.

The undercut works because the performance difference between fresh and worn tires can be substantial, often 1 to 3 seconds per lap. The trick is timing: pit too early and you may run out of tire life later in the race. Pit at just the right moment and you can leap ahead of cars that were previously in front of you.

## The Overcut

The overcut is the opposite approach:

1. Car B stays out on track while Car A pits.
2. Car B benefits from "clear air" (no car ahead creating turbulence) and pushes hard on their existing tires.
3. When Car B eventually pits, they've built enough of a gap to emerge ahead of Car A.

The overcut works best when track position is critical (making overtaking difficult), when the current tires still have life left, or when the undercut advantage from fresh tires is small. It's a riskier strategy but can be effective at circuits where clean air provides a big performance advantage.

## Safety Car Windows

A Safety Car period is a strategist's best friend or worst enemy. When the Safety Car is deployed:

- The pit lane remains open (usually).
- The field bunches up, closing gaps.
- Cars can pit and rejoin the pack without losing significant time, since everyone is running at reduced speed.

This is sometimes called a "free pit stop" because the time penalty for pitting is dramatically reduced. Teams running a one-stop strategy might find themselves suddenly behind rivals who got a cheap pit stop under the Safety Car. Conversely, a well-timed Safety Car can hand a position advantage to a team that hasn't yet pitted.

The Virtual Safety Car (VSC) offers a smaller but similar advantage. Under VSC, all cars must reduce speed by roughly 30% relative to a reference time. The pit stop time loss is reduced, though not as much as under a full Safety Car.

## One-Stop vs. Two-Stop

This is the fundamental strategic question at every Grand Prix:

**One-stop strategy:**
- Minimizes time lost in the pit lane (only one stop of roughly 22-25 seconds lost).
- Requires managing tires carefully, often running at a slower pace to preserve rubber.
- Works best at circuits where overtaking is difficult and track position is paramount.

**Two-stop strategy:**
- Costs an additional 22-25 seconds in pit time.
- Allows drivers to push harder on each tire stint, running faster lap times.
- Can work out faster overall if the pace advantage on fresh tires outweighs the extra pit stop time.
- Works best at circuits with long straights and multiple overtaking zones, where passing after pit stops is realistic.

Teams run simulations before and during the race to calculate which strategy is optimal. The calculations factor in tire degradation rates, traffic, fuel load, track temperature, and the likelihood of Safety Car interventions.

## Fuel Load Considerations

Since 2010, F1 has banned refueling during races. Cars start with their full fuel load, roughly 110 kg of fuel, making them significantly heavier at the start (a fully fueled car weighs around 800 kg including the driver). As fuel burns off, the car gets lighter and faster.

This means early laps are slower and tire stress is higher due to the extra weight. Strategy must account for this: tires degrade faster in the opening stint, and some teams deliberately run longer first stints to take advantage of the lighter car later.

## The Human Element

Despite all the data and simulation, strategy is not a purely mathematical exercise. Race engineers and strategists must make real-time decisions based on incomplete information. They don't know exactly when a Safety Car might appear, what their rivals are planning, or how a driver's tires will behave on a given day.

The best strategies combine rigorous data analysis with an instinct for racing. Some of the sport's most memorable moments have come from bold strategic calls, whether it was a gamble on rain tires under clearing skies or an aggressive undercut that nobody saw coming.
